# Preface: Why This Book, Why Now {.unnumbered}

Somewhere in the months I spent writing this book, a paragraph in
Chapter 8 had to be rewritten. Between two research passes, weeks apart,
the European Union finalized a reform that moved the compliance deadline
for high-risk AI systems — the category most customs risk systems will
live in — by sixteen months. A carefully hedged "plan against both
dates" became, overnight, a settled fact with a different date. I left a
trace of that rewrite in the chapter deliberately, because it is the
most honest single illustration of the condition this book was written
for: **the leaders of customs administrations are being asked to make
decade-scale decisions on ground that moves quarterly.**

I have spent my career on the vendor side of those decisions —
building and delivering AI systems for customs administrations across
the Gulf, the Middle East, and Asia, and watching, from inside the
delivery, what actually determines whether a program produces value or
a cautionary tale. I came to customs the long way — fifteen years of
production AI across fintech, logistics, and security, a company
founded and taken through acquisition, and enough deployments in
regulated environments to have earned my skepticism honestly. That
vantage point shaped this book in three ways that I should declare
openly.

First, it made me impatient with the existing literature. The
international reports — including the World Customs Organization's own
excellent 2025 survey of AI and machine learning adoption, which this
book cites often and builds upon gratefully — describe the technology
landscape thoroughly. What they do not do, because it is not their job,
is help a Director General decide: which use case first, at what level
of autonomy, procured how, from whom, under which contract terms,
governed by what apparatus, measured against which evidence. Those are
the decisions I have watched succeed and fail, and they are the entire
subject of this book. Every chapter ends with an instrument — a
checklist, a template, a routing rule — because a book for
decision-makers should leave them holding something they can use on
Monday.

Second, it made me allergic to unverifiable claims. The vendor world I
work in produces them industrially, and the customs conference circuit
amplifies them. So this book adopted a discipline that its research
process enforced ruthlessly: every quantified claim carries its source,
its date, and — most importantly — *who stated the figure and whether
the technology behind it is precisely identified*. That discipline
produced some uncomfortable findings, including about my own home
region: several of the most celebrated modernization numbers in the
Gulf and MENA turn out to be the achievements of single windows and
process re-engineering, not of AI. I kept those findings in, because
the book's usefulness depends on the reader trusting that nothing in it
has been laundered — and because, read correctly, they are not
deflating at all. A region that has proven it can execute
national-scale digital modernization has proven exactly the discipline
that AI adoption requires. The verified record is simply waiting to be
written, by the administrations this book is for.

Third, it convinced me that the agentic turn is different in kind, not
just in degree. A scoring model that hands an officer a number and a
software agent that *acts* — queries systems, compiles files, sends
requests, closes cases — pose different governance problems, different
security problems, different workforce problems, and different
procurement problems. Most of what is being sold today under "agentic
AI" language is priced and contracted as if this difference did not
exist. The central frameworks of this book — the Autonomy Ladder, the
principle that operational decisions can transfer while accountability
never does, the signed mandate, the evidence-gated climb — exist to
make the difference governable. I did not invent their necessity; in
the year this book was written, three jurisdictions began regulating
agents as a distinct category, and every one of them converged on the
same skeleton. The problem dictates the architecture.

A word on what this book is not. It is not a technical manual — the
engineering is deliberately kept to the minimum a leader needs to
interrogate a vendor. It is not a survey — the case studies are few,
verified, and analyzed, rather than many and decorative. And it is not
neutral about everything: it takes positions — on sovereignty, on
autonomy, on the order in which things should be built — and argues
them. Where the ground is still moving, the moving parts are marked,
dated, and assigned to a watch list, because pretending otherwise would
break the book's own first rule.

The customs mandate has not changed: collect the revenue, protect the
border, facilitate the trade. What has changed is the arithmetic around
it, and the tools available to answer that arithmetic. My hope for this
book is specific: that it shortens, by months, the distance between a
leadership team's first serious conversation about AI agents and their
first governed, measured, honestly evaluated deployment — and that it
spares at least a few administrations the expensive lessons I have
watched others pay for.

Dubai, July 2026
